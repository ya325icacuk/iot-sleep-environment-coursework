\section*{Executive Summary}
{\small\color{red}\textbf{TODO:} Tweak executive summary when the paper is done.}\vspace{0.5em}

Sleep quality is a growing public health concern. Average scores among users of the Sleep Cycle app fell by 0.46\% between 2023 and 2024~\citep{sleepcycle2025}, and a survey of over 30,000 respondents across 13 countries found that people report losing nearly three nights of restorative sleep each week~\citep{resmed2025}. Environmental factors including noise, temperature, humidity, light, and air quality are all known to affect sleep~\citep{caddick2018,yasmeen2025}. However, studies that objectively assess multiple bedroom environment variables alongside sleep in real home settings remain scarce~\citep{basner2023}, and fewer still combine low-cost sensor data with consumer wearable sleep tracking. This report addresses that gap by investigating which bedroom environment conditions are most strongly associated with sleep quality, using affordable IoT hardware in a real London bedroom. It describes the sensing setup, data collection pipeline, statistical analysis, and an interactive dashboard built to present the findings.

Over 14 consecutive nights (9 to 22 February 2026), three data sources were collected: a custom sensor system logging temperature, humidity, light, and sound, hourly air quality data from the Breathe London API, and daily sleep metrics from an Ultrahuman Ring wearable. All data was filtered to a consistent 11\,pm to 9\,am sleep window and analysed using four methods: Spearman correlation to identify which factors relate to sleep quality, OLS regression to test their combined effects, a median-split comparison of the seven best versus seven worst nights, and minute-by-minute comparison of individual nights to examine how conditions changed across the night.

Across all four analyses, noise variability (the standard deviation of sound readings) had the strongest statistical association with sleep quality, with a Spearman correlation of $r = -0.80$ ($p < 0.01$). A regression model combining noise variability with humidity explained 64.3\% of the variance in sleep score (Adj~$R^2 = 0.643$), though noise variability was the only predictor that reached statistical significance in that model. Humidity ($r = -0.70$), mean noise level ($r = -0.69$), and temperature range ($r = -0.56$) also showed statistically significant Spearman correlations with sleep score individually. Light, PM2.5, and NO\textsubscript{2} showed no statistically significant Spearman correlations with sleep quality.

Based on the above findings, reducing noise variability through strategies such as earplugs, white noise, or improved window insulation is the most practical change suggested by this dataset. The findings are based on one participant over 14 winter nights and should be treated as indicative rather than generalisable.

\subsection{Introduction and Objectives}

Sleep quality has a well-documented impact on cognitive performance, physical health, and daily productivity~\citep{medic2017}. A range of bedroom environment factors can disrupt sleep, and they do so through different mechanisms. Noise, particularly from road traffic, affects sleep architecture and triggers physiological stress responses~\citep{muzet2007,halperin2014}. Temperature and humidity influence sleep through thermoregulation, since core body cooling is a critical step in falling asleep and high bedroom temperature or humidity can interfere with this process~\citep{caddick2018}. Light exposure suppresses melatonin secretion and shifts circadian timing~\citep{yasmeen2025}. Outdoor air pollutants including PM2.5 and NO\textsubscript{2} have also been associated with increased risk of sleep disorders~\citep{zhao2024}. While each of these links is individually documented, studies that objectively assess multiple bedroom environment variables alongside sleep in real home settings remain scarce~\citep{basner2023}, and fewer still combine low-cost sensor data with consumer wearable sleep tracking.

This project investigates the relationship between bedroom environment conditions and sleep quality over 14 consecutive nights (9 to 22 February 2026). It has three objectives:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item To identify which bedroom environment conditions are most strongly associated with sleep quality.
    \item To explore whether these associations hold when multiple conditions are considered together.
    \item To compare how bedroom conditions differ between good and poor sleep nights.
\end{enumerate}

The study is motivated by two factors. First, low-cost IoT hardware now makes personal environment monitoring even more feasible without specialist equipment. A microcontroller board and a handful of sensors costing under \pounds30 can continuously log temperature, humidity, light, and noise throughout the night. Second, the study location, a central London flat facing a busy road, provides a naturalistic setting where noise, air quality, and other conditions vary meaningfully from night to night. Together, these make it possible to monitor multiple bedroom environment variables alongside sleep quality in a real home setting, an area where relatively few studies have been conducted. While the study is based on a single participant over 14 nights, it provides suggestive results on which bedroom conditions matter most for sleep and demonstrates an affordable, replicable approach to home-based sleep environment monitoring.

The Arduino sketch computes both sound average and sound peak each minute, and both are logged to the CSV. During analysis, only sound average was retained as it represents the sustained noise level during each one-minute interval, which is more relevant to sleep disruption than the momentary spikes captured by sound peak.

\textbf{Source 2: External air quality.} The Horseferry Road sensor (site code BL0046) was selected from the Breathe London network due to its proximity to the study location.

\textbf{Source 3: Personal sleep metrics.} Sleep data was collected using an Ultrahuman Ring Air wearable device worn each night. The ring uses photoplethysmography (PPG) and accelerometer sensors to track sleep stages and produces a daily summary including sleep score (0--100), total sleep, awake time, deep sleep, REM sleep, and light sleep (all in minutes). The sleep score is a proprietary composite metric computed by Ultrahuman's algorithm; the exact formula is not published, so it is treated as a ``black box'' measure in this study. To aid interpretation in the dashboard, sleep scores are grouped into three quality tiers: green ($\geq$80), gold (70--79), and red ($<$70).

\subsubsection{System Design and Limitations}

\textbf{Physical installation.} The Heltec board and all three sensors were mounted on a breadboard placed on the bedside table, approximately 0.5\,m from the pillow and 2\,m from the bedroom window facing Horse Ferry Road. The microphone was oriented towards the window (the primary noise source) to maximise sensitivity to external sound events. The DHT11 sensor was positioned away from the laptop's exhaust fan to avoid heat contamination of temperature readings. The photoresistor was left unshielded to capture ambient room light conditions. A single USB cable connected the board to the laptop for both power and serial data transfer. As all sensors are at a single bedside location, the readings represent conditions at that position rather than across the entire room. The wiring diagram and a photograph of the setup are provided in Appendices~A and~B.

\subsection{Data Collection and Storage/Communications Process}

\textbf{Arduino sketch workflow.} The Arduino sketch running on the Heltec board reads all sensors every minute. Sound is sampled continuously at 5\,ms intervals ($\sim$12,000 samples per minute); the average and peak values are computed from these samples over each one-minute window. Temperature, humidity, and light are read once per minute. Each minute, the sketch outputs a single CSV-formatted line over USB serial.

\textbf{Serial logging.} A Python script running in a Jupyter notebook on the laptop captures the serial output. Timestamps are generated on the laptop rather than the microcontroller, avoiding the complexity of WiFi/NTP synchronisation, which was unnecessary given that the Ultrahuman Ring provides daily (not timestamped) sleep metrics. Each row in the CSV contains six fields: \texttt{timestamp}, \texttt{temperature\_c}, \texttt{humidity\_pct}, \texttt{light\_detected}, \texttt{sound\_avg}, and \texttt{sound\_peak}. Each night's data is saved as a date-stamped CSV file (e.g.\ \texttt{2026-02-09\_sleep\_data.csv}) in the data folder. Over the 14-night study, this produced 14 files of approximately 600 rows each. After the collection period, all files were combined into a single \texttt{bedroom\_sensors.csv} for analysis.

\textbf{Night-assignment logic.} Because the sleep window spans midnight, each reading must be assigned to a ``night'' for cross-source merging. The rule applied is: if a reading's hour is $\geq$12 (evening), it belongs to that date's night; if $<$12 (morning), it belongs to the previous date's night. For example, a reading at 2026-02-10 01:30 belongs to the night of 9~February. This same logic is applied to the air quality data and serves as the join key when merging all three sources into the nightly summary dataset.

\textbf{Air quality collection.} Air quality data was retrieved in bulk from the Breathe London API after the two-week collection period completed, rather than in real-time during each night, to avoid the risk of missed API calls during overnight sessions. The API was found to timeout (HTTP 504) when requesting large date ranges in a single call, so the data was fetched in two weekly chunks with a 15-second pause between requests. The API returns data in long format (one row per measurement per species), which was transformed to wide format (one row per hour) with columns for the hour, PM2.5, and NO\textsubscript{2}. Only readings within the sleep window were retained, and the result was saved as \texttt{breathe\_london\_air\_quality.csv}.

\textbf{Sleep data collection.} The Ultrahuman data was downloaded manually from the app at the end of the collection period and saved as \texttt{ultrahuman\_sleep\_data.csv}. Each row corresponds to one night, with the date used as the key to join with the nightly sensor and air quality summaries.

\textbf{Sampling rate justification.} The one-minute logging interval balances temporal granularity with storage efficiency. Bedroom temperature and humidity change slowly, making sub-minute resolution unnecessary. The 5\,ms internal sound sampling captures transient noise events (traffic, sirens, door slams) that would be missed at one-minute polling; these are then aggregated to minute-level summaries for logging. Air quality data is hourly, which is the maximum resolution available from the Breathe London API. Sleep metrics are single daily values, reflecting the output granularity of the Ultrahuman Ring.

\textbf{Reliability and anomalies.} The primary reliability challenge was macOS power management: the laptop entered sleep mode during the first test night, cutting off serial capture after approximately two hours. This was resolved by installing the Amphetamine application to prevent sleep while the notebook kernel was active, and was tested over two full nights before the study began. The Breathe London API timeout issue (HTTP 504 on large date ranges) was resolved by splitting requests into weekly chunks as described in Section~1.3. No serial disconnections or significant data gaps were observed during the 14-night study period.

\textbf{Latency.} Sensor capture is near real-time via USB serial. Air quality data was retrieved retrospectively after the study. Sleep data was exported the morning after each night.

\textbf{Limitations.} The DHT11 has low precision ($\pm2$\,\textdegree C, $\pm5$\%\,RH) and a slow response time. The photoresistor is uncalibrated with a non-linear response, producing binary output only. The KY-038 provides no decibel reference, so sound values are relative analog readings (0--4095) rather than absolute acoustic measurements. The sensor system is fixed at a single bedside location and does not capture spatial variation within the room. The sleep window is fixed at 11\,pm to 9\,am regardless of actual bedtimes. With a single participant and 14 nights, the study has limited statistical power and no generalisability.

\subsection{Data Interaction/Visualisation/Actuation Platform}

The dashboard design was directly shaped by the analysis results presented in Section~2.2. To make this section self-contained, the key findings are previewed here: the analysis identified four environment features with statistically significant correlations to sleep score, specifically noise variability ($r = -0.80$), humidity ($r = -0.70$), noise level ($r = -0.69$), and temperature range ($r = -0.56$). Three other factors (light, PM2.5, NO\textsubscript{2}) showed no significant association. These findings determined which variables are featured prominently in the dashboard and which appear only in contextual views.

\textbf{Platform and deployment.} The dashboard, named RestNet, was built using Streamlit with Plotly for interactive charting, and is deployed at \url{https://sleep-monitor.streamlit.app}. The application and data files are hosted in a public GitHub repository and deployed automatically via Streamlit Community Cloud. The dashboard reads the same CSV files produced in Part~1 directly from the repository.

\textbf{UX rationale.} Key design decisions and their reasoning are as follows. A \textbf{dark theme} was chosen because the primary user context is reviewing sleep data, often in the evening or at night; a dark interface reduces eye strain and feels contextually appropriate, with a warm coral accent (\texttt{\#E8937A}) providing contrast without harsh brightness. \textbf{Section-specific colour coding} (gold for sleep overview, turquoise for bedroom environment, rose for air quality, purple for insights) allows the user to instantly identify which data domain they are viewing, reducing cognitive load when scrolling between sections. A \textbf{three-page structure} was adopted rather than a single scrolling page because 14 nights of data across multiple sensor types and sources would overwhelm the user. Page~1 (Sleep Dashboard) answers ``how did I sleep this fortnight?'', Page~2 (Night Explorer) answers ``what happened on a specific night?'', and Page~3 (My Sleep Insights) answers ``what should I change?'', forming a progressive disclosure hierarchy matching distinct user intents. A \textbf{consistent sleep-score colour mapping} (green $\geq$80, gold 70--79, red $<$70) is applied to bars, dots, and cards across all charts, so the user can visually scan any chart and immediately see whether good or poor sleep nights cluster at certain values. \textbf{Full-word labelling} replaced abbreviations throughout (e.g.\ ``Average'' not ``Avg'', ``Noise Variability'' not ``Std Dev''), targeting a non-technical user who wants to understand their own sleep. These quality tiers are defined by this dashboard and are not official Ultrahuman classifications.

\textbf{Page 1: Sleep Dashboard} provides a two-week overview with three sections. The Sleep Overview section displays metric cards for average sleep score, average duration, and average deep sleep, followed by a nightly sleep score bar chart colour-coded by quality tier. The Bedroom Environment section shows metric cards for the four significant predictors (noise variability, humidity, noise level, temperature range) with tabbed nightly bar charts for each, colour-coded by that night's sleep score. The External Air Quality section presents PM2.5 and NO\textsubscript{2} line charts with WHO guideline reference lines (15\,\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3} for PM2.5, 25\,\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3} for NO\textsubscript{2}), with a caveat that these guidelines are based on 24-hour averages while this data covers only the sleep window.

\textbf{Page 2: Night Explorer} allows the user to select any of the 14 nights and view five sections. Sleep Architecture shows the night's score, comparison to the two-week average, and a stacked bar of sleep stages. The Bedroom Environment Timeline presents minute-by-minute line charts for temperature, humidity, and noise, plus a binary heatmap strip for light. The Air Quality Verdict displays pass/fail cards against WHO guidelines. Night at a Glance provides auto-generated insight cards for bedroom extremes (coolest/warmest hour, quietest/noisiest hour) and averages (humidity, noise, light exposure, air quality). What Was Different? compares this night's four significant predictors to personal medians using traffic-light colour coding (green = better than usual, gold = close to usual, red = worse than usual).

\textbf{Page 3: My Sleep Insights} brings all three data sources together. Best vs Worst Night shows side-by-side snapshot cards with sleep scores and bedroom conditions. The Correlation Explorer displays Spearman scatter plots for three environment predictors (noise variability, humidity, noise level) plus awake time (a sleep metric included because it strongly correlates with sleep score); temperature range, the fourth significant predictor, is not plotted here but is covered in the optimal ranges table. Your Optimal Ranges presents a median-split table comparing good vs poor nights across all four significant predictors. The Actionable Takeaway section highlights the strongest predictor and provides practical recommendations.

\subsection{Data Analytics, Inferences and Insights}

The environment variables and sleep metrics operate at different timescales. Bedroom sensors log every minute ($\sim$600 readings per night), air quality data is hourly ($\sim$10 readings per night), and sleep metrics are single daily values. These cannot be compared directly in their raw form. The analysis proceeds in five steps.

\textbf{Step 1: Nightly feature aggregation.} Raw sensor readings ($\sim$600 per night) are converted into 12 comparable nightly summary features: mean, standard deviation, and range for temperature, humidity, and noise (9 features), light exposure as a percentage of readings with light detected (1 feature), and mean PM2.5 and NO\textsubscript{2} (2 features). These are merged with the six sleep metrics from the Ultrahuman Ring into a unified dataset of 14 rows $\times$ 18 columns. Sound average is used rather than sound peak, as it represents the sustained noise level during each interval, which is more relevant to sleep disruption than momentary spikes.

\textbf{Step 2: Spearman correlation analysis.} Spearman rank correlations are computed between all 12 environment features and all 6 sleep metrics. Spearman was chosen over Pearson because normality cannot be verified with $n = 14$, and Pearson assumes normally distributed data to produce valid $p$-values~\citep{field2018}. Four environment features showed statistically significant correlations with sleep score ($p < 0.05$): noise variability ($r = -0.80$, $p < 0.01$), mean humidity ($r = -0.70$, $p < 0.01$), mean noise level ($r = -0.69$, $p < 0.01$), and temperature range ($r = -0.56$, $p = 0.037$). Both noise features were also significantly correlated with total sleep duration, awake time, and light sleep duration, making noise the most consistent predictor across all sleep metrics. Humidity showed additional effects on sleep stage composition: higher humidity was associated with less light sleep ($r = -0.71$, $p < 0.01$) but more REM sleep ($r = 0.58$, $p < 0.05$), suggesting it may affect sleep architecture, not just overall quality. Light exposure, PM2.5, and NO\textsubscript{2} showed no statistically significant correlations with any sleep metric.

\textbf{Step 3: Multiple regression (OLS).} For each of three target metrics, the two environment features with the highest absolute Spearman correlation were selected as predictors (limited to two by the rule of thumb requiring 7--10 observations per predictor~\citep{field2018}). Multicollinearity was checked against the $|r| > 0.7$ threshold~\citep{dormann2013}; all predictor pairs passed (Std Noise and Mean Humidity: $r = 0.60$; Std Noise and Mean Noise: $r = 0.57$). Table~\ref{tab:regression} summarises the results.

All three models are statistically significant ($p < 0.05$). Sleep Score has the strongest fit (Adj~$R^2 = 0.643$). Noise variability is significant or borderline significant in every model, while the second predictor (mean humidity or mean noise) does not reach significance in any model once noise variability is accounted for. Residual diagnostics include Durbin--Watson (near 2.0 indicates no autocorrelation), Jarque--Bera (does not reject normality), Breusch--Pagan (tests for heteroscedasticity), and Shapiro--Wilk (tests residual normality). With only 14 observations, all diagnostic tests have limited power.

\textbf{Step 4: Optimal conditions comparison (median split).} The 14 nights are split at the median sleep score into the top 7 (``good sleep'', scores 86--96) and bottom 7 (``poor sleep'', scores 66--82). A descriptive comparison was chosen over formal hypothesis testing because with only 7 observations per group, statistical tests have very low power and would likely return non-significant results even if real differences exist. Mean noise was 15.2 points higher on poor sleep nights (86.0 vs 70.7). Noise variability was also greater on poor nights (22.2 vs 20.3). Humidity was 5.3 percentage points higher (53.4\% vs 48.1\%). Temperature was only marginally higher on poor nights (20.6\,\textdegree C vs 20.0\,\textdegree C), a difference of 0.6\,\textdegree C that is small compared to within-group variation. Light, PM2.5, and NO\textsubscript{2} showed negligible differences between the two groups (all $<$1 unit).

\textbf{Step 5: Intra-night profiles.} The minute-by-minute sensor data for the single best and worst sleep nights (by sleep score) was plotted side by side. The worst night was warmer (mean 20.5\,\textdegree C vs 18.6\,\textdegree C), substantially more humid ($\sim$58\% vs $\sim$44\%), and characterised by more frequent high-amplitude noise spikes, particularly around 11\,pm--1\,am and again after 5\,am. The best night also had early noise spikes but settled into a quieter, more stable pattern for the rest of the night. This illustrates why noise variability (not just average level) emerged as the dominant predictor.

\textbf{Note on temporal versus aggregate cross-correlation.} Steps 2--4 use nightly aggregates (one value per night per feature), not true time-series cross-correlations. This is a necessary consequence of the data design: the sleep score is a single daily value from the Ultrahuman Ring with no minute-level breakdown, so it cannot be correlated with sensor readings at finer temporal resolution. Step~5 is the closest this study comes to temporal analysis, comparing minute-by-minute patterns on different nights and revealing that the timing and pattern of noise spikes matters. A true time-series cross-correlation (e.g.\ ``does a noise spike at 2\,am predict more awake time in the following hour?'') would require minute-level sleep stage data, which the Ultrahuman export does not provide. This is acknowledged as a limitation and identified as an avenue for future work.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Discussions on the Important Aspects of the Project}

\textbf{Key finding.} Noise variability showed the strongest statistical association with sleep quality, consistent across all four analyses: correlation, regression, group comparison, and intra-night profiling. It is the pattern of repeated disruptions (intermittent traffic spikes, door closings, sirens) rather than sustained loudness that most impacts sleep. Research suggests that noise variability disrupts sleep architecture more than constant background noise.

\textbf{Air quality interpretation.} NO\textsubscript{2} and PM2.5 stayed well within WHO guidelines (15\,\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3} for PM2.5, 25\,\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3} for NO\textsubscript{2}) throughout the study. The inability to detect an effect may reflect genuinely clean overnight air during this winter period rather than an absence of any relationship between air quality and sleep. WHO guidelines are based on 24-hour averages; this study uses overnight-only data, so comparisons are indicative rather than direct.

\textbf{Limitations.} This study has several important limitations. It involves a single participant, so findings are personalised and not generalisable. Fourteen nights provides limited statistical power; correlations may not replicate over longer periods. Data was collected in winter only, introducing seasonal bias (no summer heat, open windows, or different noise patterns). The observational design permits no causal claims; environment--sleep associations could be confounded by unmeasured factors such as stress, diet, or exercise. The light sensor is binary (no lux) and the sound sensor is uncalibrated (no dB reference), limiting comparability with published thresholds. The fixed sleep window does not account for actual bedtimes varying from 11\,pm to 9\,am. The air quality sensor is outdoors and may not reflect indoor exposure. Finally, the Ultrahuman sleep score is a proprietary composite metric whose exact algorithm is not published, so findings are dependent on Ultrahuman's internal calibration and may not correspond directly to clinically validated sleep quality measures.

\textbf{Notebook versus dashboard.} The notebook and dashboard serve complementary roles. The notebook provides statistical rigour (regression diagnostics, $p$-values, residual tests) for academic assessment. The dashboard provides accessibility and interactivity for user-facing exploration. Both use the same underlying data.

\textbf{Integration pipeline.} The current system follows a linear pipeline: hardware sensors $\rightarrow$ serial capture $\rightarrow$ CSV files $\rightarrow$ analysis (notebook) $\rightarrow$ visualisation (dashboard). There is no feedback loop: the system senses and reports but does not actuate or intervene. This was a deliberate design choice for an observational study; the goal was to understand which conditions affect sleep, not to change them mid-study (which would confound the analysis). The Heltec board's WiFi and LoRa radios were not used because USB serial was simpler and more reliable for a bedside setup with the laptop physically adjacent. Section~2.4 proposes how future iterations could close the loop with real-time alerts and smart home integration.

\textbf{Project planning and development.} The project followed eight phases: (1)~hardware assembly and sensor testing in late January 2026; (2)~Arduino sketch development in late January to early February; (3)~serial logging setup and Mac power management resolution in early February; (4)~data collection over 14 nights from 9--22 February; (5)~air quality data retrieval in late February; (6)~data analysis in late February to early March; (7)~Streamlit dashboard development in March; and (8)~report writing in March. Key challenges included the Mac sleep issue (resolved with Amphetamine), API timeouts (resolved by splitting into weekly chunks), and the sound sampling iteration (initially 1/min, revised to 5\,ms high-frequency sampling after early tests missed transient noise events). Each sensor was tested individually before integration on the shared breadboard, which identified GPIO pin requirements and ADC-capable pin assignments.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Avenues for Future Work and Potential Impact}

\textbf{Better sensors.} A BME280 would provide $\pm0.5$\,\textdegree C temperature accuracy, $\pm3$\%\,RH humidity, and barometric pressure. A BH1750 would provide calibrated lux readings, enabling comparison with published light thresholds. An INMP441 I2S digital microphone would enable frequency-domain analysis and dB calibration. A CO\textsubscript{2} sensor (e.g.\ SCD40) would serve as a ventilation proxy, since CO\textsubscript{2} buildup in closed bedrooms is a known sleep disruptor.

\textbf{Longer study and multiple participants.} A study of three or more months would capture seasonal variation, increase statistical power, and enable stronger regression models with additional predictors. Including multiple participants would enable generalisable conclusions and reveal individual differences in environmental sensitivity.

\textbf{Real-time alerts and smart home integration.} The Heltec board's WiFi or LoRa radio could be used to push notifications when conditions deteriorate (e.g.\ humidity spike, sustained noise event), triggering within-night interventions rather than next-morning retrospective analysis. This could extend to smart home integration: triggering fans, dehumidifiers, or white noise machines based on sensor thresholds derived from the optimal ranges analysis, creating a closed-loop actuation system (sense $\rightarrow$ assess $\rightarrow$ intervene $\rightarrow$ measure outcome).

\textbf{Machine learning prediction.} With sufficient data, a sleep quality classifier (e.g.\ random forest, gradient boosting) could be trained on environment features to predict tonight's expected sleep quality from current bedroom conditions.

\textbf{Broader impact.} Affordable personal sleep environment monitoring could complement clinical sleep studies, particularly in urban settings where traffic noise and air quality are persistent concerns. Open-source hardware and code could enable community-scale studies.

% ── Conclusion ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section*{Conclusion}

Section~1.1 set out three objectives: (1)~to identify which bedroom environment conditions are most strongly associated with sleep quality, (2)~to explore whether these associations hold when multiple conditions are considered together, and (3)~to compare how bedroom conditions differ between good and poor sleep nights.

\textbf{Objective~1} was addressed through Spearman correlation analysis. Four environment features showed statistically significant correlations with sleep score: noise variability ($r = -0.80$), humidity ($r = -0.70$), noise level ($r = -0.69$), and temperature range ($r = -0.56$). Light, PM2.5, and NO\textsubscript{2} showed no significant association, though the study conditions (winter, closed windows, clean air) may not have provided enough variation to test these adequately.

\textbf{Objective~2} was addressed through OLS regression. When multiple conditions were considered together, noise variability was the only factor that retained statistical significance; humidity and noise level did not reach significance once noise variability was accounted for, suggesting they are partially confounded with noise patterns rather than independent sleep disruptors. With $n = 14$, the study lacks power to disentangle more subtle interactions. A longer study would enable this.

\textbf{Objective~3} was addressed through median-split comparison and intra-night profiling. The seven poorest sleep nights had substantially higher mean noise, greater noise variability, and higher humidity than the seven best nights. Minute-by-minute comparison of the single best and worst nights confirmed that it is the pattern of repeated noise spikes, not sustained loudness, that most distinguishes good from poor sleep.

Across all four analyses, \textbf{noise variability} emerged as the factor with the strongest and most consistent association with sleep quality. The project demonstrates that affordable IoT hardware (under \pounds30) combined with public APIs and a wearable device can produce actionable, personalised sleep environment insights. Reducing noise variability, through earplugs, white noise machines, or improved window insulation, is the single most impactful change indicated by this dataset.
